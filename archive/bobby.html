Searching for Bobby Fischer Discussion - CINE025 - 6/10/2023

Conrad Hall uses low-key lighting and shadows in [Searching For Bobby Fischer] to convey suspense, and entering the unknown. I felt as though the film was quite dark, and I agree with Christopher that the projector may need to be turned up as everything we've seen on the projector has been on the dark side, even YouTube videos. The darkest scenes are usually at the meets, even in the professor's building where Bobby beats the only other guy there, and quite easily. The brightest scenes are usually outside, in the park. This shows how Josh is happiest and most 'himself' when he's at the park, around normal people. (Even if they're "homeless addicts.")

In chess games, Josh leans down and puts his chin on the table, making the chess pieces at eye level. This is how he envisions the chess pieces moving around and is able to see at as "an artform." The camera also does this, and since we see the world through the lens, we're being put at Josh's level by being on this board. The film uses close up shots quite a lot, keeping the character's faces in a tighter frame. It's a particular directing style that puts the characters first, above the environment. We also see camera work used to convey a separation between Josh and his father when he's in the rain. Not only is his father physically away from him, but all the shots we see of Josh are facing down, showing how his father isn't thinking of him, and is subconsciously placing his needs above Josh's.

I'm definitely going to rewatch this one. Apart from the entire film, I really enjoyed seeing the text "Josh went on to xyz..." at the end. It's incredibly 90s and isn't something we see today. Projector aside, the lighting was super cool in this film. I honestly wasn't even paying attention to the lighting for most of it, I was so entranced by the film. I'm going to rewatch probably this weekend and do a critical analysis.
